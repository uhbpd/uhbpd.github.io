Welcome to the Peritoneal Dialysis Service

The Peritoneal Dialysis (PD) service at University Hospitals Birmingham NHS Foundation Trust is delivered across Queen Elizabeth Hospital Birmingham (QEHB) and Birmingham Heartlands Hospital (BHH).

Our specialist multidisciplinary teams provide personalised support for people receiving peritoneal dialysis, helping patients manage their kidney care at home whilst maintaining independence and quality of life.

Whether you are exploring dialysis options, preparing to start treatment, or already receiving PD, our experienced team is here to support you every step of the way.

What is Peritoneal Dialysis?

Choosing a dialysis treatment is an important decision, and it’s natural to want an option that supports your independence, wellbeing, and day‑to‑day routine. 

Peritoneal dialysis uses the lining of your abdomen (the peritoneum) as a natural filter to clean your blood. A soft tube (catheter) allows dialysis fluid to move in and out of the abdomen, removing waste products and excess fluid.

Unlike hospital-based dialysis, PD is usually performed at home, giving many patients greater flexibility and control over treatment.

There are two different forms of PD - automated peritoneal dialysis (APD) and continuous ambulatory peritoneal dialysis (CAPD).

Why Choose Peritoneal Dialysis?

Peritoneal dialysis is a home-based treatment that offers many patients greater flexibility and independence whilst providing effective dialysis.


🏡 Home-Based Treatment

Carry out dialysis in the comfort of your own home and reduce the need for regular hospital visits.

⏰ Flexible Around Your Lifestyle

Choose treatment schedules that fit around your work, family life and daily routine.

🚗 Maintain Independence

Many patients value the freedom, confidence and control that PD provides.

✈️ Easier Travel

With planning and support, many patients can continue to travel whilst receiving treatment.

💙 Continuous Therapy

PD provides a gentle, continuous form of dialysis that works throughout the day or night.

👩‍⚕️ Specialist Support

Dedicated nurses, doctors and support staff are available to guide you throughout your treatment journey.

Support Every Step of the Way

Starting PD is a learning process, but you will not be on your own. Your PD team will provide:

  • Comprehensive training programme
  • Telephone advice and support
  • Home visits where required
  • Rapid access to specialist PD nurses
  • Ongoing reviews and education

Is Peritoneal Dialysis Right for You?

Every patient is different and the best treatment depends on your medical condition, lifestyle and personal preferences. PD may be particularly suitable if you:

  • Value independence and flexibility
  • Prefer treatment at home
  • Want to reduce hospital visits
  • Prefer to avoid needles during dialysis

Preparing for PD

Before starting peritoneal dialysis, a soft tube called a PD catheter needs to be inserted into your abdomen. This procedure is usually performed as a day case or short hospital stay.

What is a PD catheter?

A peritoneal dialysis catheter is a soft, thin silicone tube about the width of a pencil that is placed in your abdomen (tummy) for peritoneal dialysis. The catheter stays in the abdomen with the end coming out through the skin just to the side of your belly button. The catheter is used to exchange waste products out of your blood by delivering and draining fluids in the abdomen.

How do I prepare for surgery?

Preoperative appointments

Before the insertion of your peritoneal dialysis catheter you will need to attend a preoperative clinic. The surgeon or a member of the surgical team will discuss the procedure with you and answer any questions you may have. We may also conduct some blood tests (to monitor your health) and take some swabs from the nose, groin, or armpit (to check for infections like MRSA).

Preparation before the surgery

Before the surgery you will be asked to use an antiseptic body wash for five days to reduce infection risk. You will also be provided with a laxative to take before the surgery to keep your bowel empty on the date of the procedure.

Prior to the surgery you will be asked not to eat for 6-8 hours leading up to the procedure. You will be able to drink clear fluids such as water until 2 hours before the surgery. You may be requested to take a laxative prior to the procedure and some of your medications may be temporarily stopped: this will be at the discretion of your doctor.

Where will the surgery be?

The surgery will take place at the Queen Elizabeth Hospital in an operating theatre.

What will happen during surgery?

PD catheters are inserted in an operating theatre under general anaesthetic, spinal anaesthetic or local anaesthetic. General anaesthetic is most common, where you will be asleep during the procedure. At our centre, the catheter is inserted using one of two methods:

Open insertion

A small cut is made just below the umbilicus (belly button). The catheter is placed into the abdomen and positioned so that the tip sits low in the pelvis, where it drains best. It is then brought out through a separate exit site on the skin.

Keyhole (laparoscopic) insertion

A small cut is made in the right upper part of the abdomen to insert a camera. A second small cut below the belly button is used to place the catheter. The camera allows the surgeon to check the position of the catheter and place it accurately.

Your surgeon will explain which method is most suitable for you. In both operations, the catheter is tunnelled under the skin before exiting through a small opening on the abdomen. This tunnel helps reduce the risk of infection.

The operation usually takes 20-40 minutes but you should expect to be in hospital for longer due to preparation and recovery time.

What happens after surgery?

Peritoneal dialysis catheter insertion is usually a day case procedure meaning you can go home on the same day as your procedure if the team feels you are well enough.

You will need to wear a dressing for a few days and keep the wound clean and dry.

You may drive once you can comfortably perform an emergency stop, have no abdominal pain and are no longer taking strong painkillers. Try to avoid strenuous exercise and heavy lifting for up to 6 weeks to allow the wound to properly heal.

What are the risks and complications?

PD catheter insertion is a common procedure and is generally safe, but all operations carry some risk.

Common

    Mild pain or discomfort around the wounds for several days
    Bruising around the incision sites
    Temporary leakage of a small amount of blood-stained fluid from the wounds
    Constipation, which can affect catheter function

Less common

    Wound infection or infection around the catheter exit site
    Leakage of dialysis fluid around the catheter
    Blockage of the catheter, preventing fluid from draining properly
    Catheter movement (migration), which may reduce drainage and occasionally requires repositioning
    The need for a further procedure to reposition or replace the catheter

Rare

    Bleeding requiring another procedure or a blood transfusion
    Injury to the bowel, bladder or nearby blood vessels during insertion
    Infection within the abdomen (peritonitis), which usually occurs after the catheter has been used but can rarely occur soon after insertion
    Infection along the catheter or where is exits the skin
    Inability to insert the catheter safely, meaning the procedure cannot be completed

Very rare

    Encapsulating Peritoneal Sclerosis (EPS). This is a rare complication associated with long-term peritoneal dialysis rather than the catheter insertion itself. It causes scarring and thickening of the lining of the abdomen, which can affect bowel function and may require specialist treatment.

When should I seek medical advice?

Please contact your kidney team, PD team or seek urgent medical advice if you develop:

    Increasing abdominal pain
    Fever or feel generally unwell
    Redness, swelling, bleeding or discharge around the wound or catheter site
    Vomiting that does not settle
    Problems draining fluid through the catheter
    A catheter that appears to have moved or fallen out

Follow-up

You will be reviewed by the PD or kidney team after the operation. They will check that the wounds are healing, make sure the catheter is in a good position, and advise you when it is ready to use. If you have any questions before or after your procedure, please contact your renal team, PD nurse specialist or surgical team using the contact details provided by your hospital.

Travelling with Peritoneal Dialysis

Having the freedom to travel is very important for some poeple on PD. If you are planning to travel (either in the UK or abroad), please talk to one of your PD doctors or nurses. You need to plan well in advance so your dialysis fluid and supplies can be arranged at your destination.

Plan early

Talk to staff at the earliest opportunity if you are considering dialysing away from home. Check with the dialysis nurse or doctor that you are fit to travel.

Destination availability

Peritoneal dialysis fluid can be delivered to a wide range of destinations around the world. However, not all destinations are available. Please discuss your travel plans with our CAPD Fluid Coordinator Mrs Helen Flanagan on 0121 371 8704 for more information.

Travel insurance

Ensure you take out the necessary travel insurance. You may need specialist insurance companies who cover a wider range of people with long-term conditions. .

Vaccinations

Please check if any are needed for your destination prior to travelling.

Transplant waiting list

If you are on the transplant waiting list you will need to inform your transplant co-ordinator so they can temporarily suspend you from the waiting list whilst you are away.
